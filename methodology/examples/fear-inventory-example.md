# Fear Inventory Example: Finding God

**Source:** Real fear-solving worksheet from "Finding God: A Companion to the Inward Journey" by Gabriel Rymberg

*This example shows what completed Part 1 (Prepare) fear work looks like.*

---

## The Fear-Solving Framework

For each fear, work through these questions:

1. **What are you afraid might happen?** (Name the fear specifically)
2. **Consequences** - If this fear came true, what would happen?
3. **Plan** - What can you do to prevent or reduce the impact?
4. **Energy** - Can you use this fear as motivation?
5. **Benefit to me** - If I write this book, what do I gain?
6. **Who else is helped?** - Who benefits from my book?
7. **What if I quit?** - What happens if I don't write it?
8. **Decide** - Am I writing it? Yes or no.

## Why This Exercise Works

### 1. Naming the fear reduces its power

When a fear lives only in your head, it's a vague monster. Writing "I'm afraid I don't have good enough English" makes it concrete and addressable.

### 2. Consequences are usually survivable

Notice how the "consequences" column reveals that most fears, even if they came true, wouldn't be catastrophic. Embarrassment isn't death.

### 3. Plans exist for every fear

Every fear on this list has some kind of mitigation. "I won't get up" â†’ "Ask Sandra to kick me out of bed." The fear feels less overwhelming when there's a plan.

### 4. The "What if I quit" column matters

This column often holds the real motivation. "Many people like me will not be helped" cuts deeper than "I will feel proud."

### 5. The final decision is binary

Yes or no. After all the analysis, you decide: Am I doing this? Gabriel wrote "YES" for every fear. That's commitment.
